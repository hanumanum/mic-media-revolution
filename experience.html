The media became an important tool for the revolution.

There were several preconditions for this. The first was the internet. Armenia has high-speed and cheap internet with very broad coverage. A very large percentage of the population has smartphones or tablets connected to the internet, and they are active on Facebook or YouTube.

The other precondition was online streaming. Starting back in 2012, several websites in Armenia had specialized in livestreaming and had improved their technical capacity to do this over the years. Viewers watched this online live coverage of events.

Nikol Pashinyan’s live videos on Facebook were also a major source of information. People would watch him to find out what he planned to do the following day. Pashinyan had only 40,000 followers at the end of March, but a month later this number had grown to more than half a million. Not a single television channel could claim such high one-time viewership numbers.

The online channels were being “blamed” for keeping people away from the protests, with some suggesting that viewers were preferring to follow the events from a distance instead of being there. But this time, the livestreamed videos were compelling, the live feeds were magnetic.

There was a universal protest, people felt compelled to come out into the streets and join their brothers and sisters who were demonstrating in the streets.

It was also very important that people were not cut off from news updates once they stepped out into the street. Technology allowed them to stay connected while they were outside. And they started to broadcast live as well, becoming a media source for their own small numbers of followers.

This was a case where the saying “people are media” was very true indeed.

People used their phones to become broadcasters, documenters and reporters. These acts of civic journalism were a great help to the news media, which would otherwise have had difficulties finding out where Nikol Pashinyan was and what was going on, because the streets in the city were closed.

Television was defeated by online news media in this round.

The resources that the previous authorities had invested into television as a means for disseminating news or propaganda ended up providing no returns. Because they were unprepared to work in these conditions, both from a technical point of view and in terms of lacking the required skills, traditional television ended up using images from the livestream coverage of online channels. Their editorial teams were nevertheless working 24/7 during the revolution. Their journalists, camera operators and photographers were walking 20-30 km a day on average.

On April 16, when the movement gathered critical mass, there was a fear that the authorities would switch off the internet at any moment, thus severing the connection between the leader of the revolution and the people.

But the authorities did not make that move. Perhaps it was a miscalculation on their part, or perhaps they were afraid of infuriating the people and pushing them out into the streets. One of the mobile operators had even added antennas at Republic Square. But there was still not enough bandwidth. Telegram offered a solution, since it could work with low-speed connections as well. A group of people created a channel called Infocom. Information began to spread quickly on Telegram.

If you want to take a step of your own, block the street next to your home.

A creative and simple but ingenious solution, which allowed more people to be brought into play and had a major impact in a short period of time.

People had to be kept on the move. It was no coincidence that the leader of the Velvet Revolution was always walking. The slogan of “taking a step” already suggested that there was no point sitting and protesting, you had to actually do something. Something that was unusual, something that brought change but was peaceful in nature. The message of peace became a core message of the movement’s rhetoric. We will block the streets, but we will walk in peace, our hands held open.

These simple rules of behavior clearly distinguished the revolutionaries from the others. It was thanks to these rules that huge masses of people transformed from crowds to organized groups with a clear statement – whoever does not behave like us is not one of us.

Because aggressive words like “We’ll drive you out, kill you, destroy you,” were not allowed, everyone expressed their anger by blowing on vuvuzelas. Its sharp buzzing was directed at the ruling authorities.

The marches were horrifically huge, and the renowned Icelandic drum and Viking chant were a great support in regulating these groups, enthusing them and filling their opponents with fear.

Two drumbeats and a clap – boom-boom-hooh! This rhythm matches the human heartbeat. Everyone’s heart was beating in the same rhythm.

People were walking at the same pace, making the same moves. This created a massive effect of unity, power and confidence, a common energy field in which one could feel protected.

The revolutionary song proclaimed the steps of the revolutionary program.

The rock song that consisted of lyrics penned by Nikol Pashinyan contained all the relevant revolutionary instructions. The revolutionary messaging was simply repeated through this song. It started with rock, which is by itself a rebellion, a quake, a change. Then the song became a repeating loop with techno rhythms, which was well suited to a rhythmic walk on the streets, when the lyrics are no longer important. Then there was the song by Tata, which was released in time for the celebrations. And finally, there was “dukhov” (literally, “with spirit”), which brought together the broadest masses since it required no profound philosophy or intelligence.

The word “dukhov” was brought into use so that people would not be afraid of the supposed power of the authorities.

The symbols of this movement were taken from everyday life. This includes the word “dukhov”. In everyday life, when we want to inspire someone with the spirit of bravery, we tell them to just be dukhov. Over many years, Serzh Sargsyan had convinced the people that they were powerless, subordinate and that only he could act as a patron to the weak if he was so inclined. But Pashinyan said that each person had their own power. People went from being powerless to powerful, and the authorities lost any power that they had on those masses.

Serzh Sargsyan’s system did not know how to act against a strong people. Nikol Pashinyan had raised people from the depths and told them, “Dukhov – go with spirit and fight!”.

“Dukhov” became a way for each person to speak out in their own name. The word “dukhov” had a masculine nature, but it lost this character because even girls began to act “with spirit”.

They grew disobedient to their parents. Parents would mostly prohibit their young daughters from going out into the street at late hours, but they rejected that parental authority alongside their rejection of Serzh. During those days of revolution, multiple watershed moments occurred.

A political struggle was underway but it was using civic methods. It was a decentralized struggle, where each participant was becoming a new source of power. In this way, the struggle grew more open in nature and was not fully national – it accepted any kind of cultural phenomenon, or innovative contribution. Each center made their own decision on what to do.

The creative nature of that power was enriched with a national character. Komitas was played on the streets because he was a powerful cultural symbol. Carpets with national design were laid out for the girls blocking Abovyan Street, symbolizing that we were fighting as one nation. This meant that certain archetypes were also being put to work. Preparing a barbecue on Republic Square was similarly an archaic idea. Sitting down on the grass and sharing the meal was a sign of unity.

There would have been no revolution without the students’ participation.

The students secured the participation of a large number of people and brought with them a new level of dynamism, diversity and audacity. Older people are always quicker to concede because they have a broader worldview and calculate all the ramifications of their actions. They carry the heavy load of responsibility, while young people act on the spur of the moment. The youth also like to play. They took pleasure in their game of cat-and-mouse with the police.

The revolution became a national carnival.

Why did the movement gain such momentum? Perhaps for the reason, among others, that our lives are not very rich in festive celebrations. We have virtually no folk holidays that are celebrated like carnivals. And in the same way that each group has their own way to express their joy during a carnival, here too each group had their own approach to voicing their protest.

It was this decentralization that turned the movement into a national carnival. We were able to avoid any major clashes because, despite our level of modernization, we are not a very diverse nation, and we are closely-knitted through our family connections and bloodlines. There were no major clashes also because there was no external involvement.

This was a revolution to solve internal issues.

The most revolutionary action was the occupation of an official square. The rallies of our opposition parties would usually take place on Freedom Square, but Republic Square was an official space from as far back as the Soviet times – it was a location that had seen many military parades and official concerts. A very important, symbolic change occurred. The revolutionaries occupied the official square, depriving it of the content it had previously held and turning it into a national square in the true sense of the word.

Similar to Republic Square, other national symbols were also liberated, like the flag. The use of national symbols meant that a symbolic change of power was taking place.

Power was being returned and transferred without violence to the people, to whom it belonged according to the Constitution.

The people were simply demonstrating that those symbols actually belonged to them. A new meaning had been conferred to the concept of statehood.
